Written by Bill A. Pennysworth

Paid* publication on behalf of Smaug Wealth Management and Trust

Since the days of Reagan, the American people have seen decades of previously unprecedented economic growth. Handed an abysmal, failing economy (GDP was a measly $2.86 trillion) by failed President Jimmy Carter, Reagan grabbed America’s bootstraps and yanked. Over the five decades since he graced the Oval Office, due in large part to his economic acumen and political foresight, GDP has increased more than tenfold to nearly $32 trillion, and today’s society is host to a proliferation of billionaires. In fact, it is only a matter of time before it produces the world’s first trillionaire — the proud American beloved around the world, Jeff Bezos.

Many on the radical left would have you believe this was a sign of moral decay or imminent societal collapse, but this is never the case. These same leftists made these same claims about Reagan, and many still do. On the contrary, it is Jeff’s odds-defying, meteoric rise that best demonstrates the persistent and thriving American Dream. Rather than criticizing Mr. Bezos’ success, I propose we adopt and apply his methods to achieve America’s greatest dream yet: infinitely sustained economic growth.

Section 1: On Taxation

Modern leftists, the so-called “progressives,” would have you believe that they’ve been abandoned by modern politics, while the more vehement among them — the ones foaming at the mouth to argue with their more sensible relatives at Thanksgiving dinner — might even claim they were usurped. In criticizing the MAGA movement, for example, they ask questions like, “When was America great?” Of course, they ignore the obvious answer, which is the Reagan era that spawned this slogan in the first place, but can you guess what answer a progressive provides if you direct the question back at them?

It’s often one of two options: either the non-answer that America was never great, or the more telling answer that they preferred FDR. Nevermind the hateful culture of Jim Crow politics, ask them why they support FDR, and they almost always answer, “the New Deal.” That’s right, the crowd crying “racism” and “No Kings” wants nothing more than a return to the era of FDR, the longest-serving president in American history. I shouldn’t have to remind you, but FDR is the reason for the 22nd Amendment, the stop gap designed to limit would-be tyrants to two terms. In his case, a radical socialist.

Arguably the most tyrannical aspect of FDR’s reign and the cornerstone of his legacy was foundational for enabling the most beneficial aspect Reagan’s two, short terms. It is also, perhaps, the best example of the left’s delusional thinking. I’m talking, of course, about the progressive tax structure, which had top marginal tax rates approaching 100%. Now, Reagan did what he could to lessen this burden for every economic class from the bottom up. His revolutionary “Trickle Down” economic structure led to some of the greatest cultural, technological, and medical progress in world history, much less American history. Reducing tax rates across the board primed the GDP for its explosive growth, but typical leftists want you to think this was detrimental to the poor and working classes. I would argue, and I’m sure you would agree, that there’s nothing “progressive” about our tax system. Or about taxes in general.

A leftist would quote Ben Franklin, “Nothing can be said to be certain, except for death and taxes,” then explain that we simply must levy them because we have no choice; but you and I know that Franklin was arguing against taxation. Why else would we dump all that tea in the Boston Harbor if not to avoid taxes? It’s the most American thing we can do.

Now, I could go on, but I’d only belabor the point, and I don’t want to waste your time. Especially since I have so much more ground to cover. Turning our attention back to Bezos, it is well known by now that he pays less in federal income taxes than the average wage earner, but with this observation comes a crucial distinction that the left routinely overlooks either from dishonesty or genuine ignorance. In the interest of human decency, I’ll assume the latter and take this opportunity to clarify what they do not understand. Who knows, maybe some of them will even learn something.

Wealth and income are distinct, and wealth is not taxed like income. Mr. Bezos, likely to be the world’s first trillionaire, does not earn a trillion dollars as income, he possesses this much wealth due to the ever-growing value of his various business endeavors.

What “progressives” fail to understand is that increasing taxes on income will never translate to decreasing Mr. Bezos’ wealth. But it will hurt the checkbooks of tens of millions of honest, hard-working Americans by greedily taking more of their hard-earned dollars just to line the luxury suits of their least favorite politicians. Politicians who go on to use that money to purchase stocks from companies like those owned by Mr. Bezos, which only increases their share values, and therefore his wealth, in turn. It is a sad irony that these so-called progressives have been convinced that giving up their money so freely is truly for their own good, and it would be sadder still if they weren’t so wrong so loudly.

The Problem of Progressive Taxation

I can summarize the truth of this contradiction in two simple ways. While I previously touched on the first of these when I quoted Franklin, there is simply no avoiding the fact that leftists believe taxes are necessary, and so they are unnecessarily imposed on all of us. Even if I granted their belief, a significant problem immediately arises that the left refuses to answer: The question of how. That is, how are taxes to be paid? This question alone introduces a revolving door of additional questions, each more troubling than the last. For instance, here is but a sample dialogue:

Q. How are taxes to be paid?

A. By the individual according to various societal needs and their related costs.

Q. Who does the individual pay?

A. Probably a governing body.

Q. Who is the governing body?

A. A committee of people like you, me, or that neighbor three houses down who never mows his lawn.

Q. Ok. So you’re working on this committee — I assume you’re being paid?

A. Of course. I’m working, aren’t I?

Q. Who signs your check?

A. Someone else on the committee, I imagine.

Q. How much do you get paid, and who decides that?

A. The committee decides.

Q. And how does the committee get the money to pay its members?

A. By collecting it from individuals.

Q. So a committee takes money from individuals to pay itself to take money from individuals?

Now, I’m no screenwriter, so maybe that’s why I can see the flaws in this logic so clearly — after all, Hollywood isn’t exactly known for its accurate depictions of reality. But for those of us engaging this dialogue in good faith, the self-gurgitating nature of this Ouroboros we call the government is devastatingly and horrifyingly clear: It takes our money and feeds itself, sprinkling its crumbs on the floor while chanting, “Bon appetit.”

But perhaps a clearer illustration is the Orwellian nature of the term “progressive” as it specifically relates to our tax structure. Think on it for but a moment, on how it punishes success rather than rewarding it, and it becomes apparent how regressive the system truly is.

For example, why is it that someone in the lowest tax bracket pays less as a proportion of their total income than someone in a higher bracket, even just the very next one? It’s preposterous to think a burger flipper at McDonald’s should effectively take home more money per dollar earned than a doctor or a lawyer or even an insurance broker. What value does the burger flipper add to society that these other professions don’t? For the people who’ve worked — actually worked — hard enough to meaningfully benefit their communities to see considerably less than what they deserve all so someone with no ambition can have more than they deserved — well, the system seems pretty obviously biased to me, and it’s not against the poor. It’s frankly miraculous that Mr. Bezos has been able to accomplish such a monumental feat in an environment so determined to destroy him.

The problem, therefore, of progressive taxation is in its own name and the left’s abuse of linguistics. A truly progressive tax system wouldn’t look anything at all like our own. It would reward success, not punish it; marginal rates wouldn’t start at 0% and grow, they’d start at 100% and shrink.

Section 2: On Income

Given this distinction between wages and wealth, the average wage earner would have you believe that the government’s minimum wage is problematic. We’ve all heard the rallying cries from modern, self-described Communists, like Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, to raise the minimum wage from it’s already ridiculously high floor of $7.25 an hour. What started as $0.25 per hour has skyrocketed over the years, increasing by 2800% since its creation; yet there were calls as early as 2012 to more than double the current rate to $15 an hour. Now they argue that it should be even higher than that. $25, $30, $50, the greed never stops!

I’ve even seen some argue that incremental increases like these should be tied to factors like “cost of living” — you know, that made up term invented by the same liberal think tanks who endorse rampant “progressive” taxation. The same organizations who cry foul at infinite growth using other made up terms like “unsustainable.” In their own parlance and by their own admission, are they not guilty of the very same sin they’re denouncing by expecting the cost of labor, indeed the largest expense (i.e. tax) on a company’s balance sheet by far, to grow infinitely? Which is it, liberals? Because you can’t have it both ways.

And I can predict the liberal response to my amended tax structure, which is why we’ve taken this little detour to Wageland. They’ll ask something like, “Wouldn’t a 100% tax on the lowest wage earners cripple their ability to spend and therefore the economy?” Honestly, I wouldn’t know. To them, this kind of systemic, egalitarian change sounds too dangerous to try, and I can’t know how it would affect the economy without seeing it in practice. It’s never been tried before, and if we ever attempted it, they’d just as soon resort to despicable ad hominems. They’ll be calling us radical revolutionaries, and you know what? Maybe it’s high time we embrace their slander.

To answer their question, no. No, I don’t think the economy would crumble. We’ve already seen how eager the working classes are to spend, spend, spend, even when they should be saving. They’re spending more on everything from groceries to gas, and they’re relying more and more on credit to do so. They could spend less if they ate better food — nobody’s too good for beans and rice, it’s just that everyone wants to live like Mr. Bezos without living like Mr. Bezos. It’s all hot chicken and designer coffee and imported tomatoes these days. They treat red meat like it grows in a corn field, then wonder why their weekly receipts say $400, $500, even $600. To them in their endless greed, the solution is always more money.

I think the long-term answer to the greed of our consumption-minded masses is broad reform of both our regressive tax system and the minimum wage that’s bogging down our great corporations. And all of this change should stem from a shift in the mindset of the American people

Redefining Labor

The number one issue that’s holding us back as a people is our relationship to the concept of work. It’s easy to look at a highly competitive job market like our own and say that it’s flawed because 10,000 highly qualified candidates are all fighting for the same job. But the issue with this line of thinking is that it treats these roles as a scarcity, then shifts the blame to the companies actually providing them. It’s slightly tangential to the facts here, but the left even invents terms like “ghost jobs” to claim that actual listings exist merely to inflate the number of available roles rather than acknowledging their own inadequacies.

Of course, all of this is meant to manufacture outrage when the truth is that most companies would be happy to hire all 10,000 workers if only they didn’t cost so much. The problem with today’s workforce is its entitlement. Every worker believes they are owed work, yet when provided with opportunities, they turn down any that don’t meet their inflated sense of self-worth. They treat jobs like the little league sport they once played where everyone was awarded a trophy simply for showing up, or worse: existing.

We must put an end to this mindset once and for all. Jobs are not a right, they’re a privilege. The founders understood this. We’re entitled to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness — nowhere in the Declaration, the Constitution, the Bill of Rights, indeed in any of the amendments was having a job listed as a fundamental right. And in as much as it matters, there isn’t a country on Earth that says so either.

The Nu Deal

Unlike the founders, FDR did believe that having a job was a right. Among his many goals with the New Deal was to enshrine this belief in Constitutional Law. Thankfully, for all our sakes, reason prevailed. In its stead, he enacted the aforementioned twin travesties of minimum wage and aggressive progressive taxation, and as I’ve also said, these are now too ingrained in the American psyche that it would take nothing short of a miracle (like the sudden, inexplicable mass lobotomization of its least knowledgeable and most impressionable, yet most well-intended, citizens — although, as the Russians and North Koreans have proven, brain damage only strengthens the effect of propaganda in its audience, so perhaps a similar mass sterilization event would be more effective) to change the course of the future so drastically that perhaps we should consider using tools of our enemy — that is State overreach — to chart a new course from the inside out. Rather than abolishing these mechanisms, we should revise them to demonstrate the power of my proposed structure, and what better way is there than adding new jobs to the market while simultaneously driving down the national debt at an astronomical rate?

Senator Ted Cruz once proposed a federal flat tax of 10%, and while I believe his heart was in the right place (he is human, after all, no matter what the vile leftist internet trolls call him in their furry forums), Senator Cruz forgot one rule about finance: You can’t balance a checkbook by increasing, or even just maintaining, expenditures if you drastically reduce income. Ask any single mother who decides to splurge on a haircut after being laid-off so she can “look professional for her next interview” just how easy it is to pay off that $200 credit card transaction when her child’s father is her only source of income and that doesn’t even cover her rent. The truth is, she knows better, and I suspect the good Senator knows better as well, but he’s too eager. Too forward-thinking. He was imagining the future where America wasn’t trillions of dollars in debt, and his optimism is a salve that America desperately needs, but only after the gaping wound of greed has been stitched shut.

However, Senator Cruz’s flat tax model is actually an excellent transient. A point about which we can pivot, reversing the current regressive tax structure to the appropriately progressive model I suggested before. Rather than 10%, we need to target a value near the current average effective tax rates with incremental changes leading us there. From that value, we can continue the incremental changes (although by larger leaps since these will bring untold prosperity in themselves) until we hit full reversal.

This process of reversal is the first and therefore most important we must take before we can implement the truly progressive goal of making work a right. Yes, I’ve already stated that only Socialists like FDR believe in the “right to a job,” and this is where I must distinguish that “work” and “a job” are not synonymous, and if you cannot tell the difference, you might actually be among the well-intended masses for which I hope a miracle occurs. I make this distinction  because you must understand the “right to a job” carries an implicit threat that businesses must create jobs, or the government will. The government is already the single biggest employer in America (what do they even do?), and in every industry where the government competes against private companies, the government wins. Every. Single. Time. And worse, every time the government intervenes in business affairs, it sides with the people. If there is such a thing as class oppression, the only truly oppressed class is the American business class.

The “right to work,” on the other hand, is an inversion of this force of oppression. The people don’t just want jobs, they want to work. By recontextualizing FDR’s Socialist angle, we have extracted a message that more accurately captures the whims of the people and the interests of the businesses that provide for them. So while these phrasings do differ, the effects will feel the same for the layman: More available jobs.

It is on this point, on the availability of new jobs, that I want to dwell because it is ultimately the crux of my entire argument. People are hurting because they can’t find enough jobs, with some hoarding four, five, even six at a time. For a time, up until President Obama, jobs were as sacred as marriage. A union between one man and one company. A mutual service that rewarded fidelity and honored trust and integrity. People those days don’t trust their partners, and so they sneak around and seek out multiple. It’s only natural for an unfaithful marriage to end in bitterness between both parties, but even in the mechanism of divorce there are protections for the abuser. Where in the past, a father might pick up a broom at a local grocery story to help pay for his son’s new bicycle, fathers today bring their affairs to the office like a husband bringing his neighbor’s wife into his own bed. People today are career adulterers; they make a career of philandering with the workforce. It’s no wonder companies feel so cheated.

Still, they offer positions to the public knowing that they’ll be used by their workers. Unfortunately for them, they need affection too. Even if they know they’ll be hurt.

So it is my final declaration that we should open the market in such a way that it reflects the will of the people, seeing as how we can’t control them. For too long, we have neglected the monogamous needs of the business for the philandering whims of the masses, and so it is without irony that I suggest we offer a foolproof way to meet those whims: the negative minimum wage.

Final Solution

Following the reversal of the tax code, the public will surely and finally understand the suffering of the successful. Perhaps they won’t notice the immediate economic benefit of this structure, namely the rapid and aggressive pay-off of the national debt, and that’s because they’ll finally, truly understand what it means to have a hurt pocketbook. And once the debt is erased, we can finally implement the tremendous cost-saving measure I’ve just proposed.

Via a negative wage, the free market will allow companies to offer value to both its workforce and its customers like never before. Gone would be the days of job cuts and staffing shortages at your local Walmart because a negative wage means guaranteed income for the business. Under this model, goods aren’t priced, but the act of stocking, scanning, and buying them is. When a patron visits one, let’s say to obtain groceries, it is now their responsibility to arrive not at a convenient time but within a window to aid in the receipt, breakdown, and organization of goods. They take their selections to places like a register and scan them as normal, then exchange an obligation to provide a certain amount of labor.

No more picking what’s cheap because you can’t afford quality, just exchange a little time and save more in the long run!

The result is an equitable system in which the worker takes whatever they want then provides their effort as compensation. Think if it like a social debt system, then think of how it would benefit everything from your wallet to your freedom. The market can handle this kind of open relationship, and it’s time we stop pretending it can’t.

Conclusion

Over the course of this piece, I have laid out my vision for a future America where the free market is allowed to treat the public it serves in the same way the public treats it. The abuse it receives, especially from those on the left, has been overlooked for too long, and it’s time we began to reverse the damage we’ve caused. As is probably clear, I am no economist, but I am an analyst who specializes in the history of capital. I believe that in the proper hands, my proposal, this modernization of our antiquated mechanisms of both taxation and wages, would see the rare joint support of otherwise competing interests, with the Congressional, Corporate, and Common American aligning for the first time since the Boston Tea Party. This two-part proposal, first the reversal of the current tax system to one that rewards success rather than punishing it and, second, the implementation of a negative minimum wage, would usher in a new era of prosperity that would once again assert America’s place as the world’s foremost economic authority.
